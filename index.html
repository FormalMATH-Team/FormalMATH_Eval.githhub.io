Can LLMs perform formal mathematical reasoning?

Formal mathematical reasoning is a cornerstone of human intelligence and remains an open challenge for AI systems. Progress is hindered by existing benchmarks‚Äô limitations in scope and scale. We introduce FormalMATH, a large-scale Lean4 benchmark comprising 5,560 formally verified mathematical statements spanning diverse domains (algebra, statistics, calculus, number theory, and discrete mathematics) and difficulty levels (from high-school olympiads to undergraduate theorems). To address inefficiencies in manual formalization, we propose a human-in-the-loop pipeline integrating autoformalization (through specialized language models), multi-agent consistency checks, and automated proof filtering, significantly reducing annotation costs. Evaluations of state-of-the-art provers reveal critical limitations: persistent domain bias toward high-school-level algebra and number theory, reliance on over-simplified tactics, and ineffectiveness of natural language proof guidance. FormalMATH provides a robust benchmark for advancing automated formal reasoning and identifying critical weaknesses in current systems.

Introduction of FormalMATH

What is FormalMATH?

Formal mathematical reasoning (FMR) provides a rigorous axiomatic framework for the automated verification of mathematical proofs. However, due to its complexity and highly specialized nature, even top experts find it challenging to efficiently formalize large theorems. Although recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have significantly promoted progress in the field of FMR, existing mainstream mathematical benchmarks (such as MiniF2F and ProofNet) are still limited in both scale and coverage, making it difficult to comprehensively evaluate and improve the performance of LLMs in formal mathematics.

To address these limitations, we introduce FormalMATII‚Äîa large-scale, multi-domain formal mathematics benchmark based on Lean4, specifically designed to advance the application of language models in formal mathematical reasoning tasks. Our project offers three main contributions:

Large-scale, Multi-domain Benchmark Dataset: We release a benchmark containing 5,560 formally verified mathematical statements, covering a wide range of domains such as algebra, geometry, calculus, and discrete mathematics, spanning various difficulty levels. The dataset is over 22 times larger than the widely-used MiniF2F benchmark.

Efficient Human-in-the-Loop Autoformalization Pipeline: We have developed an innovative human-in-the-loop autoformalization pipeline that combines multi-model consistency checking with automated negative-proof filtering, significantly reducing manual annotation costs while achieving an automatic verification accuracy of up to 78.89%.

Comprehensive Evaluation of LLM-based Theorem Provers: Through systematic evaluation of state-of-the-art LLM-driven theorem provers, we reveal critical limitations in domain generalization and formal reasoning. We also find that, in some cases, natural language guidance can negatively impact automated proving, providing important directions for future research.

Automated Dataset Creation Pipeline

FormalMATH implements an efficient automated dataset creation pipeline, integrating advanced language models with multi-layered validation strategies. We leverage foundation and theorem-proving LLMs (such as Qwen2.5, Deepthecprover-base, GPT-4, etc.) to generate formal Lean4 statements via a best-N sampling and compile-and-filter approach. Next, semantic alignment is automatically verified using multiple powerful LLMs employing chain-of-thought back-translation. Negation-based disproof strategies and automated theorem provers are integrated to further filter out invalid statements. Finally, 12 IMO medalist-level experts conduct audit-level human verification to ensure the final dataset quality. This pipeline greatly improves efficiency, preserving 78.89% of statements at the pre-verification stage while substantially reducing manual verification efforts.






Delving Deep into Common Error Patterns of Existing Provers

Setup

We analyze and define common error patterns found in existing theorem provers, using advanced general-purpose LLMs (e.g., o4-mini) to automate error diagnosis and classification. Our method extracts verbalized features from proof failures, then assigns predefined error categories using a second prompt-driven step. We analyze 100 randomly selected failed proof attempts from each prover, and domain experts manually inspect and correct the annotations. The results, presented in a table, highlight the five most common error patterns and provide insights into proof failure modes.


Case Study

We explore several error patterns, starting with the improper use of automation tactics in Lean4 provers. Over-reliance on tactics like aesop, simp, and linarith can expand the search space and lead to non-termination or unsolvable goals. We also highlight the provers' difficulties handling complex inequalities, leading to proof failures, and redundant hypotheses that clutter the proof process. Incomplete proofs are another common issue, where provers generate unfinished proofs without properly justifying intermediate reasoning steps, leading to shallow or incomplete conclusions.
